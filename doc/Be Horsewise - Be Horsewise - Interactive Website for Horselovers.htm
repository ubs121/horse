Conformation

The term Conformation evaluates the correctness of the horse's bone structure, musculature and its body proportions in relation to each other. The shoulder, body and hind quarter should be in equal proportion, with a seamless appearance and the neck flowing into the shoulder. The Topline (poll-tail) should be a series of arches. Below, Be Horsewise describes the 'ideal' conformation and shows pictures of common faults.

However, please be aware that many very good horses have less than perfect conformation. It is often a case of 'fit for purpose'.

 
Points of the Horse:

Body:
The body (barrel) should look deep, giving the appearance of the horse having short legs. Short backs have strength and can carry weight/hold condition. Long backs are weak. The back should be level, neither dipped nor arched (Roached Back).
 
 

Hind Legs:
Should be strong and well developed. There should be a generous length from point of hip to the hock. The horse should have a strong, muscular second thigh (gaskin). The hocks should be strong and well defined. The cannon bone should be short. The hind leg viewed from the side must look bent but not excessively so. A vertical line from the point of buttock to the ground should touch the hock. Viewed from behind, a vertical line drawn from the middle of the hoof upwards should go through the middle of the hock. The horse should show well muscled buttocks.

 

Forelegs:
The horse should have a muscular forearm, broad across the top from front to elbow and tapered to the knee. If viewed from the front the forearm should look muscled on the outside and flat on the inside. Knees should be large and flat and not appear bent forwards or backwards (over at the knee/ back at the knee). the cannon bone should be short. The fetlock joint should look smooth and pear shaped, not round like an apple. Pasterns should have an angle of 45 degrees and not be too long.



Feet:
The horse should have two pairs of hooves, in proportion to itself. The wall of the foot should follow the angle of the pastern, ideally 45 degrees. The hoof should have large frogs and a concave sole, with strong, bulbous heels.
 
                                

Chest:
From the front the chest should look deep and broad to allow room for the heart and lungs to expand. This also gives the horse a wider base and thus he is less likely to brush (knock his foreleg with the opposite shoe). A very wide chest can lead to the forelegs being set too far back and the horse looking bossomy.

Shoulders & Withers:
The shoulder blade should have the same slope as the pastern, ideally 45 degrees, and be of generous proportions. The wither should be well defined and at least level/higher than the level of the hind quarters.
 
                 


Neck:
Is the balancing pole for the horse and should have an arc from poll to withers and look longer on the top than the bottom, flowing from the shoulder. It should be narrow enough in the throat to allow flexion.
 

Head:
In proportion to the horse, having a strong defined jaw line and a kind eye. Nostrils should be large and high, and the mouth long enough to comfortably accommodate a bit.
